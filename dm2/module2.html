Module 02

Fundamentals & Definitions

To build complex networks, we must first agree on the language. In this module, we rigorously define what a graph is, explore the fundamental "Handshaking Lemma," and categorize special types of graphs.

The Graph Definition

Intuitively, a graph is a bunch of dots connected by lines. Formally, we speak of sets.

Definition 2.1: Simple Graph A graph \( G = (V, E) \) consists of:
  • A non-empty set of vertices (or nodes) \( V \).
  • A set of edges \( E \), where each edge is an unordered pair of distinct vertices \( \{u, v\} \).
The number of vertices \( n = |V| \) is the order of the graph.
The number of edges \( m = |E| \) is the size of the graph.

If the edges have direction (arrows), we call it a Directed Graph (Digraph). If we allow multiple edges between the same two nodes, or loops (edges from a node to itself), we call it a Multigraph. Unless stated otherwise, we assume our graphs are simple (no loops, no multi-edges).

Degrees & The Handshaking Lemma

The degree of a vertex represents its connectivity. In a social network, this is your number of friends.

Definition 2.2: Degree The degree of a vertex \( v \), denoted \( d(v) \) or \( \deg(v) \), is the number of edges incident to it.

Note: In a multigraph, a self-loop contributes 2 to the degree.
Theorem 2.1: The Handshaking Lemma For any graph \( G = (V, E) \), the sum of the degrees of the vertices is exactly twice the number of edges: \[ \sum_{v \in V} d(v) = 2|E| \]
Why "Handshaking"? Imagine a party. If several people shake hands, each handshake involves two people. If you count how many times each person shook a hand and sum them up, you will have counted every handshake exactly twice.

Corollary: The number of vertices with an odd degree must be even.

Bipartite Graphs

A graph is bipartite if its vertices can be divided into two disjoint sets, \( V_1 \) and \( V_2 \), such that every edge connects a vertex in \( V_1 \) to one in \( V_2 \).

Theorem 2.2: Bipartite Characterization A graph is bipartite if and only if it contains no odd cycles.

This leads to a simple algorithm to check for bipartiteness: Pick a node and color it Blue. Color its neighbors Red. Color their neighbors Blue. If you ever try to color a neighbor of a Blue node Blue (or Red node Red), the graph is not bipartite.
