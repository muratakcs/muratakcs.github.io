01. Appetizer: History & Applications

Before we dive into definitions and theorems, let's explore where Graph Theory came from and why it dominates the modern world of computing.

The Seven Bridges of Königsberg

Graph theory was born in 1736 when the mathematician Leonhard Euler solved a famous problem concerning the city of Königsberg (now Kaliningrad). The city was set on both sides of the Pregel River and included two large islands which were connected to each other and the mainland by seven bridges.

The Problem: Is it possible to devise a walk through the city that crosses each of those seven bridges exactly once?
Figure 1: Abstract representation of the 7 bridges.

Euler realized that the physical layout didn't matter—only the connections. He simplified the map into a set of nodes (land masses) and edges (bridges). This abstraction was the first "graph." He proved that such a walk is impossible because the graph contains more than two nodes with an odd number of connections (odd degree).

Hamilton and the Icosian Game

While Euler focused on edges, Sir William Rowan Hamilton (1805–1865) focused on vertices. Famous for discovering Quaternions (i² = j² = k² = -1), he also invented the "Icosian Game" in 1859.

The goal was to find a path along the edges of a dodecahedron that visits every vertex exactly once and returns to the start. This is now known as the Hamiltonian Cycle problem. unlike Euler's problem (which is easy to solve efficiently), finding a Hamiltonian cycle is computationally difficult (NP-Complete) for general graphs.

Modern Applications: Community Detection

Today, graph theory is fundamental to data science. One major application is Community Detection. In social networks, biological systems, or citation networks, we often want to find groups of nodes that are densely connected internally but sparsely connected to the rest of the network.

Ranking the Web

Perhaps the most lucrative application of graph theory is PageRank (used by Google). The web is treated as a massive directed graph:

  • Nodes: Web pages.
  • Directed Edges: Hyperlinks.

A link from Page A to Page B is interpreted as a "vote" for Page B. However, not all votes are equal; a vote from a high-quality page counts more. This recursive definition is solved using linear algebra on the graph's adjacency matrix.

Similarly, your GPS system uses graph algorithms (like Dijkstra's Algorithm) to find the shortest path between two nodes in a road network, where edge weights represent distance or traffic time.
